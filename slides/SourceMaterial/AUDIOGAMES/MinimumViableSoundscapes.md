# Minimum viable soundscapes: Appraising the pedagogical utility of audiogames

A major dilemma in media production pedagogy is the determination of minimum viable products, and this dilemma is especially prevalent for videogames. In popular culture, the attractiveness and prestige of a videogame normally result in significant part from the game’s detailed visual bespoke interactive assets, which require time-consuming, repetitive labour to design. The games that students have most often become accustomed to play before entering media production programs and game studies classes are usually the most expensive to produce, but the expense of triple-A-style gamemaking militates for hierarchically specialized labour disciplines that would undermine the diverse transferability of skills that postsecondarystudents need.

The dilemma is this: For their classes to make the most widely recognizable of good games, instructors would have to exploit students as workers; yet, if students do not make good-enough games in postsecondary production classes, academic programmes lose the authority by which they make crucial, prosocial interventions into game culture and popular culture more broadly. We need to assign our students minimum viable products that have aesthetic value without miring students in prematurely specializing workflows.

This paper addresses the dilemma by shifting emphasis from the video to the audio mode. What are the benefits of audio-first design pedagogy? My intuition is that the smaller storage requirements of audio assets correspond with more easily adjustable workflows and thus better teaching scenarios while still allowing students to produce media whose standard of refinement matches some aspects of the most popular games.

Per the themes of the conference, I contextualize the tradition of audio production as obsolescent within the newer format of the interactive arts. As old media continually become the content of the new, so the obsolescence of assets has value in the pedagogy of their vectors—interactive media. In other words, as a technique, digital audio is extremely mature, thus easing asset production.

I use a critical walkthrough (Light, Burgess, and Duguay, 2018) of the frameworks FMOD and Wwise as game-engine addons that allow audio-first thinking, contextualized with a literature review of audio accessibility features (see Ford-Williams et al., n.d.).

Findings might change for this work-in-progress; however, I am finding that, by emphasizing the timeline-based editing paradigm of fixed-media audio composition, the most popular current game audio frameworks struggle to represent soundscapes as interactive spaces. Moreover, the most mainstream accessibility features for blind players tend to limit gameplay exploration by streamlining movement in the “direction of story progression” (e.g., DeadeyeJediBob, 2024), repeating the timeline theme prevalent in audio design. Likewise, although AI-supported synthetic voices show promise (Fritsch et al., 2025), audio description is most established for linear scenes (IGDA GASIG, 2023). Such a linearizing effect might explain why audio games like *P**ap**a **Sangr**e* and *The** Night**jar* have remained unusual despite their affordances for accessibility (Kirke, 2018). Therefore, an underappreciated niche exists for exploratory audiogames, games that use audio to fully support environmental storytelling beyond directing navigational guidance.

## Outtakes and Concepts

A gap occurs between visually dominated game engines and timeline-focused audio engines, and this gap is a barrier for audio-first gamemaking.

Among existing audiogames, a variety of different methods exist for using audio as a mode of control and interface; however, the sparseness of this variety makes audiogaming gimmicky, devoid of canonical approaches. Consequently, audiogames have not actualized their conceptual potential for accessibility and pedagogical utility.
